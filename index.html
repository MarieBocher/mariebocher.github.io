About me

In Short...

I am a post-doctoral fellow at ETH Zurich, Switzerland. I am interested in data assimilation for geodynamical problems . I am also involved in projects that aim at improving equal opportunity whithin the Earth science community.

Here is my CV .

Research Direction

I develop data assimilation methods for geodynamics : I combine physical models of the dynamics of the solid Earth with observations to reconstruct its history. My ultimate goal is to build and validate robust techniques that reconstruct the Earth’s mantle and crust history at different time- and length-scales, in an integrated manner.

I started by working at the scale of the whole mantle circulation and its relation to long-term surface tectonics. During my PhD, I navigated between the data assimilation community , traditionally centered on applications to ocean and atmosphere dynamics, and the geodynamic modeling community , to transfer knowledge and experience between those and build an innovative framework for mantle circulation reconstruction . As a postdoc, I apply these skills on a different major topic of solid Earth dynamics: the study of the seismic cycle. I develop data assimilation tools to build a new way of estimating the stress state and mechanical properties of Earthquake-prone regions , and, hopefully, in the long term, contribute to earthquake hazard assessment.

Research projects

We live in an exciting time for geodynamics! On one side, physics-based numerical models of the solid Earth dynamics are becoming realistic enough to be compared to the complexity of the natural deformation observed at the surface of the Earth at various space- and time- scales. On the other side, instruments recording the dynamics of the solid Earth (from seismographs to spatial geodesy instruments) provide an ever larger amount of data to interpret and reconcile with each other. Data assimilation methods provide a statistically-consistent framework to combine these sources of information (time series of observations and a dynamical model) and, with the increasing computational power available, allow us to tackle questions that could only be answered partially before: How predictive are our dynamical models? From a time series of observations and our knowledge of the system’s physics, can we estimate the unobserved part of the system? How well?

I am currently working on 3 different topics. All of them focus on method development to reconstruct the evolution of the solid Earth, but they target different length- and time-scales: global mantle convection for the first one, the seismic cycle at subduction zones for the second one and global plate tectonics for the third one. For all these projects, I use sequential data assimilation methods. These are a range of techniques developped originally for ocean and atmosphere dynamics, but are adaptable to any problem requiring the combination of a time series of observations with a dynamical model.

Mantle Circulation

I use the mantle convection code StagYY (Tackley et al 1993) and built (and still develop) a data assimilation framework around it (Bocher at al. 2016, Bocher et al. 2018). The idea is to assimilate plate tectonics reconstructions into a mantle convection evolution while:

  • Allowing the interaction between the interior dynamics and surface tectonics to develop according to the physics of mantle convection with plates (ie: we don't want to use plate tectonics reconstructions as velocity boundary conditions).
  • Taking into account the uncertainties on Plate tectonics reconstructions.
  • Estimating the uncertainties on the final reconstruction.

Activism

Although we have made progress, the Earth sciences community is still far from representing the diversity of society. I try to do my part by bringing awareness on everyday sexism in academia, and by proposing initiatives to the European Geophysical Union to improve equality and diversity.

Project Did this really happen ?!

In January 2017, we lauched a blog on everyday sexism in academia. With seven other colleagues, we collect real stories about everyday sexist comments or interactions that happened in the lab or at conferences and transform them into comics, thanks to the artistic talent of one of our member, Alice Adenis.
